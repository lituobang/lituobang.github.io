Historical Context: University Systems and Academic Rules

Due to a general lack of understanding among the public regarding the historical development and operational mechanisms of the international and domestic academic communities, it is necessary to introduce the fundamental background rooted in legal and historical structures.

The Origin of the European University System

Ancient Roman civilization originated from Ancient Greek civilization, which in turn originated from Ancient Babylonian civilization. The Code of Hammurabi remains the earliest relatively complete written code of law known to date. In the societal structures of the Roman Republic and the Ancient Greek city-states, the social status of Consuls/Magistrates exceeded that of the Nobility, and the social status of Scholars—those responsible for exploring truth—was lower than both. Consuls typically simultaneously served as judges and administrative chiefs.

The Roman Republic transitioned into the Roman Empire in $27$ BC. Around the $4^{th}$ century AD, Constantine I established Christianity as the state religion, transforming the state into a theocracy. From that point until the Renaissance, the social structure in Europe was typically Clergy > Nobility > Merchants. The role of the clergy essentially replaced the role of the judge previously held by the Consuls, which is the origin of the modern separation of administrative and judicial powers. The clergy, having combined law and religion, developed a series of canon laws, and were therefore typically well-versed in Roman law. During this period, some members of the clergy often utilized their professional convenience to undertake the work of scholars. Consequently, although members of the clergy were not always scholars, most had profound legal backgrounds, and scholars were usually members of the clergy, also possessing deep legal backgrounds. The academic development fostered by the church led to the establishment of the earliest universities in Italy and France during the $11^{th}$ and $12^{th}$ centuries.

The Academic System Under Challenge

The academic community has seen a rapid expansion of personnel, resulting in a large number of researchers who lack legal training and an understanding of traditional academic institutions. Meanwhile, the restrictive power of the culture of honor is continuously weakening. Many scholars today are only familiar with the technical methods of their own research fields but lack knowledge of the institutional foundations of the academic community as a whole.

This is particularly true in science, technology, and emerging interdisciplinary fields, where many researchers receive "technical training" but lack "institutional training." They may possess the ability to operate experiments but have not received training in academic norms. This has led some of them to attempt to challenge institutions rooted in Roman law, which have been in use for nearly a thousand years, created countless brilliant achievements, and gave birth to industrial civilization.

The Attack on Representative Works

Traditionally, scientists are known for their representative works (e.g., Einstein with Relativity; Newton with Universal Gravitation; Yuan Longping with Hybrid Rice; and Tu Youyou with Artemisinin). Various awards and evaluations also center on these representative works.

In recent years, however, a voice has emerged within academia suggesting that the representative work system is outdated, claiming that modern scientific achievements rely increasingly on large collective collaborations. They advocate for the promotion of academic egalitarianism—that so long as someone is listed as an author, their contribution is equal. This trend has led to various forms of co-first author and co-corresponding author designations, followed by all-first author listings, alphabetical sorting, and even suggestions for circular arrangement of authors.

The Flaw in Academic Egalitarianism

Academic egalitarianism, besides the problems commonly associated with egalitarian systems, carries an additional fatal flaw: even if a scientific result is achieved through collaboration, with the exception of a very small number of perfectly equal two-person collaborations, all other situations involve a degree of leadership, which is systematically obscured by these egalitarian practices. This leadership must be quantified.
